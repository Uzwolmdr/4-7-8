Breathing Techniques for Anxiety and Panic Attacks

 

Anxiety and panic attacks can be incredibly frightening experiences, often accompanied by physical symptoms such as a racing heart, shortness of breath, and dizziness. During these moments, it can feel like you're losing control. However, by learning to regulate your breath, you can regain a sense of calm and manage the overwhelming feelings of anxiety.

One of the most common symptoms of anxiety and panic attacks is hyperventilation, or over-breathing. This is when you breathe too quickly and deeply, which can lead to a decrease in carbon dioxide in your blood, causing further symptoms like lightheadedness and tingling in your hands and feet. The key to managing anxiety through breathwork is to slow down your breathing and restore the balance of oxygen and carbon dioxide in your body.

Here are some breathing techniques that can be particularly helpful during moments of anxiety and panic:

Pursed-Lip Breathing

This technique is simple yet powerful. It helps to slow down your breathing and provides a sense of control.

  • Inhale slowly through your nose for a count of two.
  • Pucker your lips as if you're about to whistle.
  • Exhale slowly and gently through your pursed lips for a count of four. The exhale should be twice as long as the inhale.
  • Repeat this until you feel your breathing start to slow down and a sense of calm returns.

Diaphragmatic (Belly) Breathing

This technique encourages deep, calming breaths from your diaphragm rather than shallow, anxious breaths from your chest.

  • Find a comfortable position, either sitting or lying down.
  • Place one hand on your chest and the other on your belly.
  • Inhale slowly and deeply through your nose, allowing your belly to rise. The hand on your chest should stay relatively still.
  • Exhale slowly through your mouth, feeling your belly fall.
  • Focus on the sensation of your belly rising and falling with each breath.

Grounding Breath

This technique combines breathing with a grounding exercise to bring you back to the present moment.

  • Sit in a chair with your feet flat on the floor.
  • Close your eyes and bring your attention to your breath.
  • As you inhale, feel your feet connecting with the ground. Imagine roots growing from the soles of your feet into the earth.
  • As you exhale, imagine releasing any tension or fear down through your roots into the ground.
  • Continue this visualization with each breath, feeling yourself become more and more grounded and centered.

It's important to practice these breathing techniques when you're feeling calm so that you can easily access them when you're feeling anxious. By making these exercises a regular part of your routine, you can build your resilience to anxiety and feel more empowered to manage panic attacks when they arise.
